It's only feburary and this year I have already particpated in GoatHacks, TreeHacks, and MakeMIT. My team won [Best Hardware](https://devpost.com/software/change-thing-door-thing-see-thing) at GoatHacks and [2nd in Entertainment](https://devpost.com/software/super-amazing-cool-volumetric-display) at MakeMIT.

With the advent of vibe coding, hackathons are now different, and it is only hardware hackathons that carry the true hackathon spirit now.

-----

To tell you the truth, I don't think vibe coding is really only an exasperator of a greater underlying problem I have with hackathons. I've always believed that hackathons are:
1. A time to try building something new from scratch
2. A place to meet other people making
3. A chance to express your technical skill and design ability

I'm concerened that point 3. has been compeltely upended by vibe coding. Vibe coding has essentially allowed participants to ability to fake their ability to technically accomplish something. 

> "Quick, come up with an idea that sounds good on paper, is difficult to do in practice and have our model vibe code a half baked demo that barely functions because we couldn't do any better."

Combine this with a general inability of judges to measure the merit of ideas, and we get a problem: Projects that would not work if fully fleshed out, made by people who couldn't fully flesh it out, are being awarded. This is the exact opposite signals we should be sending the people who created these types of projects. Not even in the sense that the idea has no merit, but simply that awarding these types of projects provides a false sense of complacency and confidence in bad ideas and low technical ability.
